# Does anyone know what my ADHD brain means when it says "just five more minutes" at 3am? 😅🧠

HOOK (0–30 seconds)

Okay. It's 3am.

My brain just leaned over and whispered, "Just five more minutes."

Five more minutes of what?? I wasn't doing anything. I was trying to SLEEP.

But now I'm reorganizing my entire life philosophy, planning a business I'll never start, and remembering that one time I said "you too" when the waiter said "enjoy your meal."

[B-ROLL]

Can anyone translate what my ADHD brain actually means when it says "five more minutes"? Because I don't think it knows either.

Stick around. Because by the end of this video, you're going to understand exactly why this happens — and one thing you can do tonight to actually shut it off.

SETUP (30–90 seconds)

Here's the situation.

You're exhausted. You've been tired all day. You yawned through your entire afternoon.

And then the second your head hits the pillow?

Your brain goes: "SHOWTIME."

[B-ROLL]

Suddenly you're wide awake. Ideas everywhere. Random memories. Tomorrow's to-do list. That thing someone said in 2014.

And every time you almost fall asleep, that little voice says, "Just five more minutes." So you scroll. Or you think. And five minutes becomes ninety.

This is called "revenge bedtime procrastination," and it is SO common in ADHD brains it's almost a personality trait.

So today I'm going to break down what's actually happening in there. Not to shame you. To explain it. And I'll give you an actual tool at the end that works with your brain instead of fighting it.

Let's decode this.

BODY

Section 1: Your brain finally got quiet — and it hates that

Here's the first thing to understand.

All day, your brain is bombarded. Notifications. People. Tasks. Noise. Your ADHD brain is under-stimulated at its core, so it constantly reaches for input.

Then bedtime comes. The lights go off. The world goes quiet.

And your brain panics. Because now there's no stimulation coming in — so it MAKES its own.

[B-ROLL]

Think of it like a hyperactive dog that finally has the whole house to itself. It's not going to sleep. It's going to find your shoes and destroy them.

Your brain says "five more minutes" because silence feels like withdrawal. It's craving one more hit of stimulation before it lets go.

So that scrolling? That random deep thought? That's not a discipline problem. That's a dopamine-starved brain self-medicating.

Which brings us to the next piece.

Section 2: "Five more minutes" is your dopamine talking, not your logic

Let's talk about that specific phrase. "Just five more minutes."

Your logical brain knows you need sleep. It's begging you.

But there's a louder voice — the part of your brain hunting for dopamine. And nighttime is when that hunt gets desperate, because you spent all your willpower during the day.

[B-ROLL]

Here's the analogy. Imagine your self-control is a phone battery. ADHD brains drain it faster because everything takes more effort — focusing, masking, remembering, redirecting.

So by 3am? You're at 4% battery. And a 4% battery cannot fight a craving.

That's why "five more minutes" wins. It's not that you're weak. It's that you're negotiating at your absolute lowest point of the day, against your brain's strongest impulse.

You're never going to out-discipline a dead battery. You have to change the game earlier.

But first — let's talk about the memories.

Section 3: Why your brain plays your greatest cringe hits at 3am

Okay, this one's personal.

Why does your brain wait until 3am to remind you of that embarrassing thing you did years ago?

Because during the day, you're too busy to feel it. At night, there's nothing else. So your unfiltered emotional brain steps forward.

[B-ROLL]

ADHD brains tend to have a strong emotional memory and something called rejection sensitivity. Little social moments get stored with big emotional labels attached.

Think of it like your brain has a filing system, but instead of filing memories by date, it files them by feeling. And at night, when it's cleaning up, it opens the "cringe" drawer and goes, "Should we look at these? Yes. All of them."

So that's not random torture. It's a brain processing unresolved emotional data — at the worst possible time.

The good news? Once you know it's just a filing glitch, it loses some of its power. It's not a warning. It's not a sign you're a bad person. It's just an old file with a scary label.

Now — let's actually fix this.

Section 4: The real reason "five more minutes" always turns into ninety

Here's the trap.

ADHD brains struggle with something called time blindness. We genuinely cannot feel time passing accurately.

So when your brain says "five more minutes," it truly believes it. It's not lying to you. It just has no idea how long five minutes actually is.

[B-ROLL]

It's like asking someone with no sense of direction, "Are we close?" and they confidently say "Yeah, super close" — while completely lost.

That's why five minutes becomes an hour and a half. Your brain isn't tracking it. There's no internal alarm going off saying "hey, it's been forty minutes."

So the solution isn't willpower. It's putting the clock OUTSIDE your brain. Because your brain cannot be trusted to hold time.

Which leads to the one thing I actually want you to try tonight.

Section 5: The one tool that actually works — the "brain dump landing pad"

Here's the thing that helped me most.

Half the reason your brain won't shut up is that it's scared you'll forget something important. The idea. The task. The thing you have to say tomorrow.

So it keeps repeating it, louder and louder, like a kid tugging your sleeve.

[B-ROLL]

The fix: give your brain a landing pad.

Keep a notebook or a notes app right by your bed. When a thought pops up — the business idea, the to-do, the random reminder — write it down. One line. Then let it go.

You're basically telling your brain: "I got it. It's saved. You can stop guarding it."

Here's the analogy. Your brain won't put a plate down until it knows it won't shatter. The notebook is the counter. Once the plate is safely down, your hands are free — and your brain can finally relax.

Pair that with one more thing: give yourself a wind-down buffer. Twenty minutes before bed where you let your brain have low-stakes stimulation on PURPOSE. Music. A calm show. Doodling. Something that satisfies the craving before you turn the lights off — so it's not clawing for a hit at 3am.

You're not fighting your brain. You're feeding it early so it doesn't riot later.

CTA (final 30 seconds)

So the next time your brain whispers "just five more minutes," you'll know exactly what it means.

It means: "I'm under-stimulated, low on dopamine, terrified I'll forget something, and I have no idea what time it is."

And now you know how to answer it.
