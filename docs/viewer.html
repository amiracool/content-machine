# ADHD & Starting a New Hobby Every 3 Days Like It's a Personality 🤣

HOOK (0–30 seconds)

So I own a ukulele.

I also own watercolor paints. A bread-making kit. Roller skates. A "learn Italian in 30 days" workbook that made it to day two.

And a pottery wheel. Don't ask about the pottery wheel.

[B-ROLL]

Here's the thing nobody tells you. If you have ADHD, this isn't a lack of commitment. It's not you being flaky. It's actually your brain doing exactly what it was built to do.

And once you understand *why* — you can finally stop hating yourself for it.

Stick with me. This is going to reframe your entire relationship with your unfinished projects.

SETUP (30–90 seconds)

Let me guess your situation.

You saw a video. Someone made resin art, or learned to crochet, or started running. And a voice in your head went: *"That's it. That's who I am now. This is my thing."*

[B-ROLL]

So you bought the supplies. All of them. Probably the expensive starter kit, not the beginner one.

For about three days, it was your whole personality. You told people about it. You reorganized your life around it.

And then... nothing. The spark just left. You didn't decide to quit. It just quietly stopped mattering.

Now you've got a closet full of evidence that you "never finish anything." And you feel like garbage about it.

Here's what I want to give you in the next few minutes. First — the actual brain science of why this happens, so you can stop blaming your character. Second — three ways to work *with* this instead of against it, so your hobbies stop feeling like proof you're broken.

Let's get into it.

BODY

Section 1: It's not boredom — it's a dopamine cliff

Your brain isn't chasing the hobby. It's chasing the *novelty*.

ADHD brains have a different relationship with dopamine — the chemical that makes things feel worth doing. New things flood you with it. A new hobby is basically a dopamine fire hose. Everything is fresh, uncertain, and exciting, and your brain lights up like a slot machine hitting jackpot.

[B-ROLL]

But here's the trap. Once you get past the beginner phase — once you kind of know how it works — the novelty runs out. And when novelty runs out, so does the dopamine.

Think of it like a sparkler. That first strike is dazzling. But sparklers aren't designed to burn forever. When it fizzles, you didn't do anything wrong. That's just what sparklers do.

[B-ROLL]

So when people say "you just need more discipline," they're missing it completely. It's not a willpower problem. It's a fuel problem. Your fuel ran out because the *interesting* part ended.

Once you see it that way, the guilt starts to loosen. Which brings me to the second thing.

Section 2: The "hobby graveyard" is actually a skill museum

We frame all these abandoned projects as failures. A graveyard of things we gave up on.

But flip it around. Every single hobby you tried taught you *something*. You learned it. And then you kept it.

[B-ROLL]

You didn't become a master baker. But now you understand yeast. You didn't become a musician. But you can read basic chords and you understand rhythm better than you did.

I like to call it a skill museum, not a graveyard. Museums are places we're *proud* of. They hold interesting things you collected on your journey.

Here's a real example. I know someone who cycled through photography, then video editing, then graphic design, then writing. Each one lasted a couple months. She felt like a total failure — until she got a job that needed *all four*. Turns out she'd accidentally built the exact skill stack for a career she loves.

[B-ROLL]

ADHD brains are generalists. We collect wide, not deep. And in a world that rewards people who can connect different fields, that's not a bug. That's kind of a superpower.

But — and this matters — you can be intentional about it. Here's how.

Section 3: Rent the hobby, don't marry it

Most of our shame comes from *how we buy in*, not from quitting.

We go all-in on day one. We buy the professional kit. We announce it to everyone. So when the spark fades, we've spent $200 and told twelve people, and now quitting feels like a public failure.

[B-ROLL]

So try this instead. Rent the hobby before you marry it.

Borrow the equipment. Use the free version. Buy the cheap kit, not the fancy one. Do a single trial class before you commit to a ten-week course.

[B-ROLL]

Think of it like dating instead of proposing. You're allowed to try something for a week just because it's fun *right now*. That's a complete experience. It doesn't need to become a lifelong identity to have been worth it.

The goal isn't to trick yourself into finishing everything. The goal is to lower the stakes so trying stuff stays *fun* — instead of becoming another way to fail.

Which leads to the last piece, and honestly this one changed everything for me.

Section 4: Build a "rotation," not a "resolution"

Neurotypical advice says: pick one thing and stick to it forever.

For an ADHD brain, that's a prison. And prisons make us want to escape.

[B-ROLL]

So stop trying to have *one* hobby. Have a rotation.

Keep three or four things going at once. When one gets boring, you don't quit — you just switch to another one in the pile. Then weeks later, when the novelty resets, you come back to the first one and it feels fresh again.

[B-ROLL]

It's like a TV show playing episodes on shuffle. You're not abandoning the show. You're just not watching the same episode on loop until you hate it.

I've got a guitar, a sketchbook, and a running app. I never touch all three in the same week. But over a whole year? I'm consistently doing *something*. And every one of them feels exciting when I come back, because I gave it enough space to become new again.

[B-ROLL]

That's the reframe. Consistency doesn't have to mean daily. It can mean returning. And returning is something your brain is actually good at when the pressure's off.

Section 5: Separate the hobby from your worth

Here's the part I really want you to hear.

You are not the sum of your finished projects.

[B-ROLL]

The story you've been told is that quitting means you're lazy, unreliable, that something's wrong with you. And you've probably believed it for years. Every abandoned hobby became another piece of evidence in a case against yourself.

But that whole case was built on the wrong rules. It was built on a neurotypical scoring system — one that measures your brain on a game it was never designed to play.

You are allowed to enjoy something and then let it go. You are allowed to be a person of many interests. You are allowed to try roller skating for two weeks purely because it made you laugh.

[B-ROLL]

That's not failure. That's a full life. That's curiosity. And curiosity is one of the most beautiful things an ADHD brain has to offer.

So the next time you feel that pull toward a shiny new thing — go ahead. Enjoy it. Rent it. Rotate it. And drop the guilt at the door.
