# The Masking Tax: Why "Easy" Social Chats Leave You Wrecked

HOOK (0–30 seconds)

Why do neurodivergent folks feel completely exhausted after "easy" social chats?

You just talked to a coworker for ten minutes. Nothing dramatic. No conflict. And now you need to lie down in a dark room.

Meanwhile everyone else acts like small talk is *free.*

It's not free. Not for you. You've been paying something called the masking tax — and nobody warned you it was coming out of your account.

[B-ROLL]

Let's talk about it. Because once you understand this, you're going to stop blaming yourself for being "lazy" or "antisocial."

SETUP (30–90 seconds)

Here's the situation you're probably living in.

You go to work. You go to the party. You do the group chat. On the outside, you look totally fine. Maybe even bubbly.

But inside? You're running twelve background programs at once. You're monitoring your face. Your tone. Your eye contact. Whether you're talking too much. Whether you interrupted. Whether that joke landed.

[B-ROLL]

And by the time you get home, you're wrecked. You cancel plans. You go silent. And people call you "flaky."

Here's what I want you to walk away with today. By the end of this video, you'll understand exactly *why* social interaction drains you differently, what masking actually costs your brain, and three specific ways to lower the tax so you stop burning out.

This isn't an excuse. It's an explanation. There's a difference.

BODY

Section 1: Masking Is a Full-Time Job You Never Applied For

Masking is when you consciously hide your natural neurodivergent traits to appear "normal."

You suppress the stim. You force the eye contact. You script your responses before you say them. You laugh a beat late because you're checking if laughing is the right move.

Think of it like this. Most people walk into a conversation like walking into their own living room. Comfortable. Automatic.

[B-ROLL]

You walk into every conversation like you're on a first date at a fancy restaurant. You're watching your posture. Reading the menu of social rules. Trying not to knock over the water glass.

[B-ROLL]

That constant self-monitoring uses real cognitive fuel. Your brain isn't relaxing during that chat. It's *working.*

So of course you're tired. You didn't just have a conversation. You performed one.

Which brings me to the part that surprises people the most.

Section 2: Your Brain Doesn't Know the Difference Between "Small Talk" and "Stress"

Here's the wild part. For a lot of neurodivergent brains, low-stakes social stuff triggers a genuine stress response.

The heart rate ticks up. The nervous system goes on alert. Your body is quietly preparing like there's a threat in the room.

[B-ROLL]

There's no lion. It's just Karen from accounting asking about your weekend. But your body didn't get that memo.

Think of your social battery like a phone with a broken efficiency setting. A regular phone dims the screen and closes apps to save power. Your phone runs everything at full brightness the whole time.

[B-ROLL]

So the same ten-minute conversation costs you three times the energy it costs someone else. Same activity. Wildly different price.

And this is why "just push through it" doesn't work long term. You're not weak. You're paying more for the same thing.

Now — here's what happens when the bill comes due.

Section 3: The Delayed Crash Nobody Sees

This is the sneaky part. The exhaustion often doesn't hit *during* the interaction. It hits *after.*

You held it together at the meeting. You were charming at dinner. And then two hours later, you're staring at a wall unable to reply to a single text.

[B-ROLL]

This delay is why people misunderstand you. They saw you fine at 3pm, so they don't believe you're fried at 6pm.

It's like running a marathon and then being told you can't be tired because "you were smiling at mile ten."

[B-ROLL]

The masking tax gets charged with interest. And that crash — the shutdowns, the canceled plans, the sudden need for silence — that's the invoice arriving late.

Understanding this changes everything. Because now you can plan for the bill instead of getting blindsided by it.

Which is exactly what we're going to do next.

Section 4: Three Ways to Lower the Masking Tax

Okay. Here's the practical part. You can't eliminate masking overnight, but you can lower the cost. Three moves.

**One: Build in recovery *before* you crash, not after.**

Schedule quiet time on the calendar *after* social events, the same way you'd schedule the event itself. Don't wait for the burnout. Prevent it.

[B-ROLL]

Think of it like charging your phone before it hits zero. Way easier than letting it die and scrambling.

Two: Find your "unmasking zones."

These are the people and places where you don't have to perform. Maybe it's one friend. Maybe it's your dog. Maybe it's texting instead of calling.

[B-ROLL]

The more real rest you get in those zones, the more fuel you have for the situations where masking still feels necessary.

Three: Stop grading your social performance.

That replay in your head at 11pm — "did I talk too much, was that weird" — that's overtime the tax keeps charging even after you're home.

[B-ROLL]

When you catch the replay, name it. "That's the masking tax, not the truth." You don't owe anyone a perfect social scorecard.

None of these are magic. But together, they turn a full-price day into a discount one. And over a week? That adds up to your life back.
