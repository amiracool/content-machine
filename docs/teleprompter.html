ADHD & Starting a New Hobby Every Single Week 🎨🎸📸

HOOK (0–30 seconds)

You bought the guitar.

You bought the beginner's book. The little clip-on tuner. The nice strap.

And you played it. For four days. Passionately.

[B-ROLL]

Now it lives next to the pottery wheel you used twice, the running shoes you wore once, and the "learn Italian in 30 days" app that's been notifying you for eight months.

[B-ROLL]

If your closet looks like a graveyard of "this is going to be my whole personality" phases... don't scroll. This one's for you. Because I promise it's not what you think it is.

[B-ROLL]

SETUP (30–90 seconds)

Here's the story you've been telling yourself.

You think you're lazy. You think you don't have follow-through. You think there's something wrong with you because everyone else picks one thing and gets good at it, and you're over here with a ukulele, a bread starter, and a half-finished cross-stitch that says "live laug—"

[B-ROLL]

You've probably even said it out loud. "I just never finish anything."

And every time you start something new, there's this little voice going, "You KNOW you're gonna quit this too."

But that story is wrong. Like, factually wrong.

[B-ROLL]

So in the next few minutes, I'm going to show you what's actually happening in the ADHD brain when you start a new hobby every week. Why it's not a character flaw. And — this is the important part — how to work WITH it, so you stop feeling guilty and start actually enjoying the thing you love the most: starting.

Let's get into it.

BODY

Section 1: You're Not Chasing Hobbies. You're Chasing Dopamine.

Here's the truth: the ADHD brain runs low on dopamine. And a brand-new hobby is basically a dopamine slot machine.

[B-ROLL]

Think about the moment you decide to learn something new. Watercolor painting. Rock climbing. Making sourdough. That decision feels *amazing.* You're buzzing. You're watching tutorials at 1am. You're imagining the future version of you who's incredible at this.

That buzz? That's a dopamine hit. And your brain is genuinely starved for it.

So it's not that you love hobbies. It's that you love the *beginning.* The beginning is where all the novelty lives. And novelty is the fuel your brain actually needs to function.

[B-ROLL]

That's why week one feels electric and week three feels like a chore. Nothing's wrong with you. The dopamine just... ran out. The newness wore off.

So the first thing I need you to hear is this: you were never chasing the guitar. You were chasing how the guitar made your brain *feel.* Which brings me to something nobody tells you.

Section 2: "Quitting" Is Actually Data — And It's Making You Interesting

We treat abandoned hobbies like failures. Little tombstones of who we were supposed to become.

But flip it around.

[B-ROLL]

Every hobby you tried taught you something. The pottery taught you you actually love working with your hands. The guitar taught you you're more into music than you thought. The Italian app taught you you like the *idea* of travel more than grammar.

[B-ROLL]

You're not collecting failures. You're collecting *data* about yourself. And you're accidentally building a ridiculously wide range of skills that most people never touch.

Here's the analogy. A specialist is a person who dug one incredibly deep well. Amazing. Useful. But you? You're a person who's mapped an entire landscape. You know a little about a hundred things. And when life hands you a random problem, you're the person who goes, "Oh, funny story, I did pottery once, I actually know how to fix that."

[B-ROLL]

The world is obsessed with mastery. But the world runs on connectors — people who can see how things fit together because they've *touched* a bit of everything.

That's you. That's literally a superpower dressed up as a flaw. So the goal isn't to stop starting. The goal is to stop feeling *bad* about it.

Section 3: The Real Problem Isn't Quitting — It's the Guilt Tax

Okay, here's where it actually hurts.

Starting hobbies isn't the problem. The problem is the guilt you pay every single time.

[B-ROLL]

You start something new and you feel this weird shame. Because past-you spent forty bucks on paints you barely used. So now, every new hobby comes pre-loaded with a debt. You're not just starting fresh — you're starting while dragging around every "unfinished" thing behind you.

[B-ROLL]

And that guilt is the thing that actually kills your joy. Not the ADHD. The guilt.

Because here's what happens. You want to try beekeeping — okay, maybe not beekeeping, but you get it — and instead of enjoying the excitement, this voice goes, "Why? You'll just quit. You always quit. Remember the guitar?"

[B-ROLL]

That voice isn't protecting you. It's just stealing the one thing you're genuinely great at: being a beginner. Being curious. Diving in.

So let me give you permission right now. You are allowed to try something for two weeks and stop. That's not failure. That's a complete experience. You got the dopamine, you learned something, you moved on. Nature is healing.

Section 4: How to Actually Work With This Brain (The Fun Part)

Alright, let's make this practical. Because I don't want you to just feel better — I want you to feel *free.* Three shifts.

One: Rent, don't buy.

For your next hobby, borrow it. Rent the gear. Get the cheapest possible version. Use the library. Try a single free class before you drop two hundred dollars on equipment.

[B-ROLL]

This kills the guilt at the source. If you spent five dollars instead of a hundred, there's no debt to feel bad about when you move on. You get the dopamine of starting without the financial guilt trap.

Two: Build a "hobby graveyard" you're actually proud of.

Get one box. One shelf. Everything you've tried goes there. But rename it. It's not a graveyard. It's your "curiosity collection."

[B-ROLL]

When you see it, don't think "look at everything I quit." Think "look at everything I was brave enough to try." Most people are too scared to start ANY of these. You started all of them.

Three: Let hobbies rotate back.

Here's the secret nobody tells you. ADHD interest isn't linear. It's a carousel.

[B-ROLL]

The guitar you "quit"? You might pick it up again in eight months and suddenly love it. You didn't quit. You paused. Interests don't end for us — they cycle. So stop throwing things out the second the buzz fades. Just... park it. It might come back around when your brain is ready for that hit again.

[B-ROLL]

When you stop forcing yourself to be a specialist and let yourself be a beautiful chaotic generalist, the whole thing gets fun again. You stop starting hobbies to prove something. You start them because you're curious. And curiosity is the best part of being you.

Section 5: The Reframe That Changes Everything

Let me leave you with this.

[B-ROLL]

For years I thought the goal was to "finally stick with something." Like there was a finish line and I kept dropping out of the race.

But the finish line was a lie. There is no rule that says a hobby only counts if you do it forever.

[B-ROLL]

You are allowed to be a person who loves *learning* more than *mastering.* You're allowed to have a shelf full of half-explored worlds. You're allowed to be the friend who's tried everything and can talk to anyone about anything.

That's not a broken specialist. That's a fully functioning *you.*
