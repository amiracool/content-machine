ADHD & "I put it somewhere safe" — narrator: it was never found again 🤣

HOOK (0–30 seconds)

You did the responsible thing.

You had something important — your passport, your keys, that one charger that actually works — and you thought, "I'm not gonna lose this. I'm gonna put it somewhere *safe*."

[B-ROLL]

And that, my friend, was the last time you ever saw it.

[B-ROLL]

Because here's the ADHD twist nobody warns you about: the safer the spot, the more permanently that item vanishes from existence. If you've ever torn apart your entire home looking for something *you* hid from *yourself* — stick around. This isn't a you problem. It's a brain thing. And I can prove it.

SETUP (30–90 seconds)

Let's name what's actually happening.

You're not careless. You're not "scatterbrained." You didn't lose the thing because you don't care about it. You lost it *because* you cared. You put in extra effort. You made a *decision*. A special spot. A safe spot.

[B-ROLL]

And then your brain quietly deleted the entire memory of that decision.

Here's the frustrating part. When you ask people for help, they say things like "retrace your steps" or "just put things back where they belong." Cool. Great advice. For a brain that *stores* that information. Yours doesn't work like that — and that's not a flaw in your character.

So in the next few minutes I'm gonna show you exactly *why* your safe spots betray you, what's happening in your brain when you hide-and-lose, and — the part you actually want — three changes that make your stuff stop disappearing. Not "try harder." Actual systems built for the way your brain really works.

Let's get into it.

BODY

Section 1: The "safe spot" is a memory with no anchor

Here's the core problem. When you put something in an *unusual* spot, your brain has to store two things: the object, and the brand-new location.

Neurotypical brains tend to auto-save that combo. ADHD working memory? It saves the file... and forgets to hit "save location."

[B-ROLL]

Think of it like taking a photo without geotagging it. You have proof the object exists. You have zero idea where the photo was taken. That's your brain at the exact moment you go, "Wait, where did I put my passport?"

And it gets worse. A "safe spot" is by definition somewhere *new* and *out of the ordinary* — which means there's no routine, no repetition, no habit to fall back on. It's a one-time memory that got no rehearsal. Of course it evaporated.

[B-ROLL]

So the safe spot isn't safe. It's just *rare*. And rare is exactly what your memory can't hold onto. Which brings us to the sneakiest part of this whole thing.

Section 2: Out of sight isn't "out of mind" — it's "erased from the universe"

You've heard "out of sight, out of mind." For ADHD brains, that phrase is basically a documentary.

There's a thing called object permanence — the understanding that stuff still exists even when you can't see it. Babies develop it. But a lot of ADHDers describe *object permanence for daily life* being... unreliable. If you can't see it, part of your brain genuinely acts like it stopped existing.

[B-ROLL]

So when you tuck your keys inside a drawer, inside a pouch, inside a box, "to keep them safe" — you didn't protect them. You buried them so deep that your out-of-sight brain filed them under *gone forever.*

Here's the concrete example everyone knows: the fridge. You put leftovers in a container in the back. You do not see them. Therefore they do not exist. Therefore you order takeout. Therefore you find them three weeks later as a science experiment.

[B-ROLL]

The same exact mechanic applies to your important stuff. The more hidden, the more "safe," the more invisible — and invisible, to your brain, means *deleted.*

So what actually works? Not hiding things better. The opposite.

Section 3: The fix is "one home per thing" — and it has to be visible

Here's your first real system. Every important object gets *one* home. Not a rotating cast of clever safe spots. One. Boring. Predictable. Home.

Your keys don't go "somewhere safe." They go in the bowl by the door. Always the bowl. Even when the bowl feels dumb. *Especially* when the bowl feels dumb.

[B-ROLL]

Why does this work when "put it away" never did? Because you're not relying on memory anymore. You're relying on *habit and repetition* — the two things your brain actually can build. You do it enough times, and dropping the keys in the bowl becomes automatic. No decision required. No memory required. No "safe spot" required.

And the bowl has to be *visible.* Remember section two? Out of sight, out of universe. So the home for a thing should be in your line of sight, not tucked in a drawer. Open shelves. Clear containers. Hooks on the wall. Let your stuff *stare at you.*

[B-ROLL]

Think of it like a restaurant kitchen. Nothing important is hidden. Tools hang where the cook can grab them without thinking. That's not messy — that's designed for a brain that's moving fast and can't afford to "remember where things go."

One home. Visible. Boring. Now let's handle the times you *can't* use the home.

Section 4: When you break the rule, leave a breadcrumb

Real life happens. Sometimes you *have* to put the passport somewhere different — a trip, a guest coming over, whatever. You're gonna break your "one home" rule. That's fine. But when you break it, you leave a breadcrumb.

The breadcrumb is simple: the moment you hide something in a non-home spot, you *immediately* create an external record. Not in your head — your head is the whole problem.

[B-ROLL]

Fastest version? Take a photo of exactly where you put it. The open drawer, the item sitting there. Done. Now you don't need to remember — you just need to check your camera roll, which you look at 400 times a day anyway.

[B-ROLL]

Even better: text yourself. "Passport is in the blue suitcase side pocket." Send it. Now it's searchable. Future-you, mid-panic, types "passport" and gets the answer instead of transforming into a tornado of ripped-open drawers.

Think of it like a hiking trail. You don't memorize the forest. You follow the markers someone left so you don't get lost. Be that someone. Leave markers for the version of you who's already forgotten.

[B-ROLL]

And the reason this beats "just remember" is it plays to your strength — your ADHD brain is *amazing* at reacting to a cue in front of you and *terrible* at retrieving a cue from nowhere. So put the cue in front of you.

Section 5: Reduce the number of things that CAN get lost

Last one, and it's the cheat code. The best way to stop losing things is to have fewer things to lose, and fewer decisions to make.

Duplicates are your friend. Chargers, scissors, phone cables, glasses — the cheap stuff you lose constantly? Buy multiples. Put one in every zone where you actually use it. Now "where's my charger" stops being a crisis, because there's one right there.

[B-ROLL]

This isn't wasteful. Calculate the hours you've spent searching. Calculate the replacements you *already* bought in a panic. You've been buying duplicates the whole time — just chaotically, at full price, at midnight.

[B-ROLL]

And for the truly critical items — passport, IDs, spare key — go the other direction: give them a *locked, dedicated, never-touched* home. Not a "safe spot" you invent on the fly. A permanent one you set up *once,* when you're calm, and never move again. A little box that only ever holds important documents. That's the difference between "safe spot" and "safe *system*." A safe spot is a decision you make in the moment and forget. A safe system already exists before you need it.

[B-ROLL]

See the pattern across all of this? We're not asking your brain to remember better. We're building the world *around* the brain you actually have. Visible homes. External breadcrumbs. Fewer decisions. That's not lowering the bar. That's engineering.

Here's the thing I need you to hear. You losing your stuff was never a discipline problem. It was a *design* problem — you were using neurotypical advice on an ADHD brain, and then blaming yourself when it didn't work. It was never gonna work. Now you've got systems that actually match how you're wired.
